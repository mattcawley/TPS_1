Leather, bracelet, or rubber: choosing the right strap for your watch

By the Timepiece Scout team  ·  2 Apr 2026  ·  4 min read

The strap is the most underestimated element of watch buying. People spend weeks deliberating over a reference and then accept whatever strap it comes on without a second thought. This is a mistake. The same watch on different straps can look like two entirely different objects.

Here is a practical guide to the main options and when each is appropriate.

Leather: the default for a reason

A leather strap is the most versatile choice for most watches in most situations. It reads as more refined than a bracelet and more considered than rubber. For a dress watch, it is the only correct option. For a sporty watch, a leather strap can add a civilised quality that the watch itself might not suggest.

The quality of leather varies enormously. A stitched alligator strap from a quality maker will outlast a cheap sewn calfskin option many times over. Expect to pay between £50 and £200 for a strap that is genuinely good. It is money well spent.

The downside of leather is water. A leather strap soaked regularly in sweat or water will deteriorate quickly. For a watch you plan to wear swimming or during serious exercise, leather is not practical.

Steel bracelet: robust and timeless

A well-made steel bracelet elevates a sports watch considerably. The Rolex Jubilee, the Omega Seamaster bracelet, the Tudor Pelagos bracelet: these are not afterthoughts. They are integral to the character of the watch. A bracelet also makes sense for daily wear in all weather, since metal does not deteriorate with moisture the way leather does.

The quality of bracelets varies as dramatically as the quality of straps. A bracelet with poor tolerances will develop play and rattle within a year. A tight, well-finished bracelet will last decades. Feel the bracelet in the shop before you buy. If it rattles when you shake it, it is not good enough.

Rubber and FKM: for the serious athlete

Rubber straps, and specifically FKM (fluoroelastomer) straps, are the correct choice for anyone who plans to swim, dive, or exercise regularly while wearing their watch. They are impervious to water and sweat, easy to clean, and durable. They also look excellent on the right watch.

FKM straps from makers like Rubber B or the in-house options offered by Omega and Tudor are of genuinely high quality and fit the lug geometry of the watch precisely. A badly fitting rubber strap looks worse than almost anything else. A well-fitting one looks purposeful and correct.

NATO and fabric: casual and versatile

A NATO strap is a single piece of nylon or polyester that threads through both spring bars and across the back of the watch. It is inexpensive, extremely durable, and looks excellent on vintage and vintage-inspired watches. Tudor fits fabric straps to several of its Black Bay models from the factory, which tells you something about how seriously the format should be taken.

The casual character of a NATO strap means it is rarely appropriate with formal dress, but for weekends, travel, and anything outdoors, it is hard to beat.

The practical answer

If you are buying a watch that will do one thing, buy the strap that suits that thing. If you are buying a watch that will do everything, buy it on a leather strap and then add a bracelet and a NATO. Changing straps takes thirty seconds with a spring bar tool and costs almost nothing. It is one of the genuine pleasures of watch ownership.
