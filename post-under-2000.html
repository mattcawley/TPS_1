The best watches under £2,000 in 2026: a curated shortlist

By the Timepiece Scout team  ·  19 Apr 2026  ·  7 min read

The sub-two-thousand pound market has never been stronger. For a long time it was a compromise zone: decent movements, acceptable finishing, nothing to get excited about. That has changed. The brands competing for attention in this range are now making watches that would have been considered remarkable at twice the price a decade ago.

What follows is not a comprehensive list. It is a curated one. These are references we would confidently recommend to someone spending real money for the first time.

Tudor Black Bay 36 (from £1,890)

The 36mm version of Tudor’s flagship dive watch is, in this team’s view, the finest watch available under £2,000. The MT5400 in-house movement delivers COSC accuracy and a 70-hour power reserve. The case, at 36mm, is the ideal size for most wrists and reads as genuinely elegant rather than sporting. Available on leather, fabric, or steel. The snowflake hands and domed crystal give it a character that no other watch at this price replicates.

Longines Master Collection (from £1,400)

Longines makes watches of extraordinary quality for the price. The Master Collection uses COSC-certified movements, offers a range of dial configurations including moonphase and chronograph, and presents impeccably. The leather strap options in particular are excellent. If you want a dress watch that will last thirty years, this is the answer under two thousand pounds.

Grand Seiko SBGW235 (approximately £1,800)

Grand Seiko is still undervalued in the UK market relative to its reputation in Japan and the United States. The SBGW235 uses a hand-winding movement with a power reserve indicator, presented in a case with the brand’s signature Zaratsu-polished finishing. The dial texture is extraordinary. Nothing at this price looks like it.

Nomos Glückäuf (from £1,200)

Nomos Glashutte makes in-house movements in Saxony and sells them at prices that make Swiss watchmakers uncomfortable. The Glückäuf, designed as a tribute to the mining heritage of the Glashutte region, uses the Epsilon calibre and presents in a clean, legible dial that works beautifully on any occasion. If minimalism is your aesthetic, Nomos is essential.

Oris Aquis Date (from £1,250)

The Oris Aquis is the choice for someone who wants a serious sports watch at this price. Water resistant to 300 metres, ETA movement, rotating bezel, unidirectional rotation, solid bracelet: it ticks every functional requirement. The colour options for the dial have expanded in recent years, and several are genuinely striking. The Date version adds a date complication without cluttering the dial.

Frederique Constant Classics (from £900)

Frederique Constant is chronically underrated. The Geneva-based brand makes in-house movements and presents them in classically proportioned cases at prices that make no sense given the quality. The Classics range, in particular, offers date, moonphase, and power reserve complications for less than most brands charge for a plain three-hander. If you want horological depth at a modest price, look here first.
