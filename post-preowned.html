Pre-owned vs new: the honest case for both sides of the argument

By the Timepiece Scout team  ·  24 Mar 2026  ·  6 min read

The pre-owned market for serious watches has never been more accessible. Platforms like Chrono24, Watchfinder, and Bob’s Watches have made it possible to buy a pre-owned Rolex, Patek, or Audemars with a degree of confidence that simply did not exist twenty years ago. Meanwhile, the new watch market offers manufacturer warranties, full traceability, and the particular pleasure of owning something that has never been worn by anyone else.

Both sides of this argument have genuine merit. Here is an honest assessment of each.

The case for buying new

When you buy new from an authorised dealer, you buy certainty. The watch has been serviced to manufacturer specification, the warranty is intact, the documentation is complete. If anything goes wrong in the first two years, the cost of repair is not yours.

You also buy simplicity. There is no provenance to verify, no seller to interrogate, no risk of an undisclosed service history or a dial that has been polished by a previous owner. You hand over money, you receive a watch, and you know exactly what you have.

For many references, buying new is also simply more practical. High-demand watches are allocated by authorised dealers. You cannot easily buy a Rolex Submariner pre-owned at retail price because it does not trade below retail price. For watches with waiting lists, buying new from the dealer with whom you have a relationship may be the only realistic route.

When you buy new, you buy certainty. When you buy pre-owned wisely, you buy value.

The case for buying pre-owned

The most compelling argument for pre-owned is price. Watches depreciate off the secondary market in ways that favour the buyer. A watch that retailed for £3,000 three years ago will typically be available pre-owned for £1,800 to £2,400 depending on condition and box and papers. That gap represents real money that can buy a better watch, or a second watch, or simply stay in your pocket.

The pre-owned market also gives you access to discontinued references. The Omega Seamaster 300M from the 1990s, the original Longines Heritage pieces, the early Tudor Black Bay with gilt indices: none of these are available new. If you want them, you buy pre-owned or you go without.

There is also a pleasure in the history. A watch that has been owned and worn carries a particular kind of character. Not every buyer values this; some find it disconcerting. But for collectors, the idea of an object with a life before you is part of the appeal.

The risks of pre-owned and how to manage them

The risks are real. Dials can be refinished, removing original patina and significantly reducing value. Movements can have undisclosed service work. Cases can be polished, softening the edges that define the character of the watch. Documentation can be forged.

The best mitigation is buying from reputable dealers who provide written warranties. Watchfinder, Watches of Switzerland pre-owned, and established independent dealers on Chrono24 with long trading histories are safer than private sellers on forums, however persuasive the listing. Ask for high-resolution photographs of the movement if they are not provided. Request the full service history.

If you are spending more than £2,000 on a pre-owned watch, consider having it inspected by an independent watchmaker before finalising the purchase. The cost is modest. The peace of mind is considerable.

Our honest recommendation

For a first serious watch, we lean toward new. The certainty is worth the premium when you do not yet know exactly what you want, and the experience of buying new from a respected dealer is one worth having. For a second or third watch, pre-owned is frequently the smarter choice. You know what you want, you know how to evaluate condition, and the savings are meaningful.
